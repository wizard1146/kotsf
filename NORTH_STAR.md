# Keeper of the Sacred Flame — North Star

> **What we are trying to recreate.** This is the design conscience for KOTSF. When a feature,
> scene, or system decision is ambiguous, it should be resolved in favour of the principles below.
> Source: an analysis of *why King of Dragon Pass endures*, adopted as our guiding philosophy.

---

## The KODP principles (the bar we're aiming for)

1. **Meaningful decision-making.** Nearly every choice has long-term consequences; few obviously
   correct answers. The player weighs risk, culture, politics, and scarce resources.
2. **Emergent storytelling.** Stories are generated per-campaign, not authored as a fixed arc.
   Players remember *their* disasters, leaders, betrayals, miracles years later.
3. **Myth as gameplay.** Myths are *mechanics*, not lore. Re-enacting sacred stories tangibly
   improves outcomes. Understanding the world's mythology directly improves performance.
4. **Deep simulation behind a simple interface.** Beneath plain screens: morale, magic, diplomacy,
   provisions, exploration, trade, war, leader personalities, reputation.
5. **No perfect information.** Advisors disagree; intelligence is incomplete; even probabilities are
   obscured. Governing a society, not solving an optimisation puzzle.
6. **Distinct personalities.** Each leader has different skills/biases. *Who* speaks or leads a rite
   often matters more than raw stats.
7. **Cultural immersion.** No generic fantasy. A coherent society where religion, honour, kinship,
   and seasonal survival drive politics.
8. **Replayability.** Random events, neighbours, quests, personalities, resources → dramatically
   different campaigns.
9. **Failure creates stories.** Losing is entertaining. A famine or failed quest becomes *history*,
   not just "game over."

**Why it still feels modern:** most "choice-based" games offer *cosmetic* decisions. KODP makes them
**systemic** — choices alter economy, military, diplomacy, magic, succession, morale, and the ending
itself. The player role-plays an entire society, not a single character.

**The central experience** is answering hard questions, not winning battles: *Raid starving neighbours
or trade with them? Which gods get your scarce sacrifices? Trust advisors or intuition? Is honour
worth survival?*

---

## What each principle means for KOTSF (our commitments)

| KODP principle | Our commitment in KOTSF |
|---|---|
| Meaningful decisions | Every scene choice writes **multiple** pressures, often in tension (gain Lore but stir the Fracture; please Red but anger Blue). **No dominant choice.** Avoid choices that are strictly best. |
| Emergent storytelling | Keep the **storylet engine** central: scenes selected by state, never a scripted campaign. The **Saga** is the artifact of *this* run. Resist authored "main quest." |
| Myth as gameplay | The **Infinity Veil / rune-quests** must be *mechanics* — re-walking runes tangibly grants Lore/Regard and unlocks options. Knowing the myth (the Codex) should help the player *win*, not just flavour. |
| Deep sim, simple UI | The 9-screen rune chrome over the pressure model is the vehicle. Keep screens **legible**; keep the simulation **deep** underneath. Never add UI complexity that isn't backed by real systemic depth, or depth the UI hides entirely. |
| No perfect information | **Advisors disagree** (the Circle's conflicting advice is a feature). Telegraph odds *in-fiction*, not as exact percentages. The one honesty exception: permanent Flame-loss is always knowingly courted (PLANNING §5a). |
| Distinct personalities | The **Circle** must matter mechanically — *who* you send into the Flame, *whose* counsel you take, *who* leads a rune-quest changes outcomes. Personalities/feuds/grief are gameplay, not set-dressing. |
| Cultural immersion | Stay inside the **Runiverse** voice — the Forgetting, the Flame, the seven Cults, Forgotten Souls. No generic fantasy. Respect the canon/invention boundary (PLANNING §1). |
| Replayability | Seeded RNG + state-driven selection + permanent loss → different campaigns. Scene pool breadth and Circle/Cult variety are the levers; **scale content** (ROADMAP Phase 1→2). |
| Failure creates stories | Losing must be **authored and memorable** — defeat states with their own copy, the Saga recording the fall, Souls remembered by name. A loss should read as a *tragedy you caused*, not a stall. |

## Litmus tests (use these when reviewing any addition)

- **The dominant-choice test:** is any option strictly best regardless of state? If so, it fails #1 — add a real cost.
- **The cosmetic-choice test:** does this decision change ≥2 systems or just flavour text? Systemic or cut it (#1, "why modern").
- **The myth test:** for any Veil/quest feature — does *understanding the lore* improve the player's outcome? If not, it's decoration, not myth-as-mechanic (#3).
- **The personality test:** would the outcome differ if a *different* Circle member acted? If never, the Circle is set-dressing (#6).
- **The saga test:** if the player lost here, would it make a story worth retelling? If it's just a stat hitting zero, author the failure (#9).
- **The honesty test:** are we faking depth with UI, or hiding real depth the player can't act on? Aim for *simple surface, deep system* (#4).

*Keep this short and used. When in doubt, optimise for **the story the player will remember**, not the
optimisation puzzle they will solve.*
